Mean temperature of the catch of {{mtc.eez}}

The mean temperature of the catch (MTC) in a given region, which is an indicator of the poleward shifts of a number (n) of exploited populations of fish and invertebrates (i), is computed for a given year (MTCyr) as from their (stable) temperature preference (Ti)

MTC yr = ∑ i=1 n Ti Ci,yr ∑ i=1 n Ci,yr

where Ciyr is the catch of i in that region in year yr, with (Ti) estimated from FishBase (www.fishbase.org). The MTC tends to increase when, for example, in the EEZ of a given country, the distribution of the local species shifts poleward (Northward or Southward, depending on which hemisphere one is) and is replaced by species from lower latitudes, which have higher preferred temperatures (Cheung et al. 2013. Nature 497: 365-368).

The year from which this replacement begins to be noticeable in the waters of {{mtc.eez}} is {{bp_year}} (see figure), but this estimate is tentative and may improve as to catch reconstructions are updated.

For this EEZ, i and Ti apply to genera, with Ti computed as the mean preferred temperature of the species of each genus computed from the preferred temperatures of the species of that genus in FishBase (www.fishbase.org). This procedure was applied to this EEZ either because the catch (or the bulk thereof) was reported at the level of genera, or because the MTC signal was clearer at the level of genera than at the level of species.

Some fish and invertebrate species may shift away from the EEZs of tropical countries, but species with higher temperature preferences do not replace them. Hence, the MTC of tropical countries and territories is not expected to increase, and their MTC time series are not shown.

Also not shown are MTC time series of non-tropical countries or territories whose catch statistics are too scattered to generate the typical warming signal, i.e., two partial MTC series with the later one exhibiting an ascending slope steeper than the earlier one.

However, the typical warming MTC signal, i.e., two partial MTC series with the later one exhibiting an ascending slope steeper than the earlier one does not occur in the following two cases:

  1. Some fish and invertebrate species may shift away from the EEZs of tropical countries, but species with higher temperature preferences do not replace them. Hence, the MTC of tropical countries and territories is not expected to increase, and their MTC time series are not shown.
  2. Some non-tropical countries or territories have catch statistics that are too uncertain and which generate a scatter of MTCyr values with no coherent trend.

We will endeavor, in the next years, to improve the taxonomic resolution of the catch statistics for the EEZ of the countries and territories in (2) such their MTC trends can be displayed. For the time being, they can’t.
